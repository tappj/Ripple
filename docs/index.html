The cheap gate

Intent is an image, not a prompt.

Text drifts — "mustard-yellow" renders differently on every call. So Ripple converges on the edit as a still image first (~20 credits a try), and only an explicitly approved keyframe unlocks video spend. That approved image then guides every Aleph call as timed keyframe guidance, so all of them aim at the same target.

The pipeline

Five stages, priced before they run.

Ingest

Drop per-shot files, or one exported sequence Ripple splits at detected scene cuts. Originals are never touched.

Hero frame

Scrub to the frame that shows the target, describe the change, iterate on cheap image drafts, approve one. ~20 cr / attempt

Plan

Shots are packed in scene order into groups that fit Aleph's window (≤30s, ≤10 cuts), each anchored to a keyframe derived from your approved edit. Full plan + credit estimate shown; nothing runs until you say so.

Propagate

Per group: concat → upload → aleph2 with timed keyframe guidance → poll with live progress → split the result back into shots at known cut offsets. 28 cr / second of input

Review

Every shot gets a playback-synced before/after wipe. Accept what holds; retry a drifted shot alone with a freshly derived keyframe — one bad shot never means re-running the scene.

Honest edges

Where it breaks, on purpose.

  • Identity jumps break propagation. Aleph assumes visual continuity across cuts; a different actor or product variant can come back inconsistent. Detection is human — that's what the review board is for. Automatic drift scoring is the obvious next build.
  • Re-split assumes preserved timing. Cut boundaries in the edited group could drift by a frame or two versus the original offsets.
  • One edit at a time. No stacking, no undo tree — edit layering is next on the list, and the data model was shaped for it.
  • Crash-safety has one narrow window. In-flight paid tasks are re-attached by task id after a restart; a crash that straddles submission itself fails loudly rather than risk a blind double-charge.
